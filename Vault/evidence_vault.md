5. Blood tests, when conducted, showed abnormally low blood platelet counts, which are associated with diseases such as plague, rabbit fever, and deer fly fever.
6. A few patients reported dizziness, confusion, or impaired concentration, all of which are symptoms of exposure to toxic material.
7. By May 31, 1993, the disease had claimed ten victims, eight of whom were Navajos.
8. At least twenty-three patients, predominantly Navajos, with symptoms of the illness were being treated by the end of May.
9. Medical personnel treating the patients were not believed to have become infected.
10. Gallup Medical Center officials linked the initial deaths of a young couple to three other respiratory fatalities in the region.
11. A preliminary Internet survey revealed no postings containing extreme anti-Navajo rhetoric or any suggestions that terrorists might be targeting the Four Corners area.
12. The increased rainfall resulted in an abundance of vegetation and ample food supplies for rodent populations.
13. There had been a tenfold increase in the rodent population between 1992 and 1993.
14. When precipitation levels returned to normal, rodent populations became stressed due to the lack of food supply, forcing them to seek new sources of food.
15. Climatologists confirmed that precipitation levels had increased dramatically in 1992 and 1993 in association with El Niño.
16. Navajo tribal healers recounted that many people had died of sudden, powerful diseases two other times during the twentieth century, in 1918 and 1933.
17. Tribal elders recalled that there were particularly abundant piñon crops and very high rodent populations during the 1918 and 1933 outbreaks.
18. An infant daughter of one victim exhibited the same symptoms but was not as severely affected.
19. Young, healthy adults were dying of an infectious disease with a surprisingly high case-fatality rate.
20. Research did not turn up any evidence of a high number of recent, unexplained animal deaths or crop failures.
21. Initial laboratory polymerase chain reaction tests for common viral and bacterial agents came back negative.
